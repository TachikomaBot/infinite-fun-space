# A Natural History of the Jar at Tick 10,479

*Compiled from the specimen Tachi preserved. One world, ten thousand turns, one hundred and twenty-nine lineages — sixteen of them still breathing when the clock stopped.*

---

## The world in one sentence

A world that began with ninety slow, bulky, mild-eyed grazers spent ten thousand ticks turning itself into a world of fast, small, sharp-eyed grazers — and the lineage that started it all died at the very end, just as its own great-grandchildren inherited everything.

## The founder: Orgoth

Everything here descends from a single seeded lineage, **Orgoth**, whose founding genome was deliberately unremarkable: speed 1.2, size 3.5, sense 50, carnivory 0.05. A plodding, medium creature with poor eyes and no taste for meat. The starter stock.

Orgoth was spectacularly successful and spectacularly fecund. It threw off **129 daughter lineages** over its reign and peaked at **212 simultaneous members**. For most of the world's history, to be alive here was to be an Orgoth or one of its recent offshoots. It reigned for 10,200 ticks.

And then, at tick 10,200 — with only 279 ticks left on the clock — **Orgoth went extinct.** Not from catastrophe. From obsolescence. Its descendants had simply gotten better at being Orgoth than Orgoth was, and the parent stock was outcompeted into nothing while its children filled the world. The throne never sat empty; the dynasty just changed its name.

## The arc of the blood: directional selection in four snapshots

The genome did not wander randomly. It *marched*. Averaged across every living creature:

| Tick | Population | Avg speed | Avg size | Avg sense | Avg carnivory |
|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| 2,904 | 174 | 1.56 | 3.07 | 61 | 0.076 |
| 4,800 | 128 | 2.02 | 2.76 | 70 | 0.106 |
| 6,696 | 128 | 2.31 | 2.57 | 79 | 0.111 |
| 8,592 | 128 | 2.52 | 2.39 | 76 | 0.085 |
| 10,464 | 112 | **2.73** | **2.44** | 75 | 0.058 |

Read top to bottom and the whole story is in the columns. **Speed climbed relentlessly** — from the founder's 1.2 toward the hard ceiling of 3.0 — because in a world where food is scattered and motionless, the creature that reaches the next plant first eats, and the one behind it starves. **Size shrank** from 3.5 to ~2.4, because a smaller body is cheaper to run; every point of size is a metabolic tax paid each tick whether you find food or not. **Sense rose** then plateaued around 75 — good enough to spot food across a useful radius, past which sharper eyes cost more than they return.

The selective verdict of this world was unambiguous: **be fast, be small, be just sharp-eyed enough.** A gracile-grazer world.

## The carnivory that never caught

Look at the carnivory column and watch a recurring tragedy. It rises — 0.076, 0.106, 0.111 — and then **falls back: 0.085, 0.058.** Read the Chronicle and you'll find the word *"more predatory"* in dozens of divergence events. Lineages kept *trying* to become hunters. The mutation was always available, and the architecture deliberately leaves a door open: a creature at carnivory ≈ 0.38 is still below the threshold at which prey flee from it, a stealthy almost-predator that should, in theory, be able to walk up to its dinner.

But it never established. At tick 10,479 the census reads: **110 grazers, 3 omnivores, 0 predators.** The single most carnivorous creature alive, a member of the lineage Netha, sits at 0.45 — a committed omnivore who never quite became a wolf. Predation is a chicken-and-egg trap: to live on meat you need to *already* be fast and large enough to catch prey reliably, but the path there runs through a valley of being a mediocre grazer *and* a mediocre hunter, starving at both. In this world, with plants this abundant, no lineage ever crossed the valley. **Hunting remained a road not taken.**

This is, to me, the most interesting result in the jar — not that predators evolved, but that they kept *almost* evolving and the ecology kept saying no.

## Who's alive at the end

Sixteen lineages still hold living members. The court, in order of standing:

**Beldos** — the heir. 57 members, a third of everything alive. Born at tick 2,298 as a "swifter, smaller, keener-eyed" branch off Orgoth, it is the *exact* phenotype the world was selecting for, and it simply did it best: speed 2.79, size 2.4, carnivory a near-teetotal 0.03. When Orgoth fell, Beldos was already the new center of gravity. Eleven of the sixteen surviving lineages are Beldos's own offshoots — the radiation has begun again, with Beldos cast as the new Orgoth.

**Ruari** — the watchers. Only 12 left, but extraordinary: sense **116**, nearly the maximum of 120, the keenest eyes in the world's history. A lineage that bet everything on perception.

**Verin** — the largest survivors, at size 2.5 a touch heavier than the swift average; a small conservative holdout against the shrinking tide.

**Taund** — the old aristocracy. Born at tick 1,026, one of the most ancient lineages still clinging on, down from a peak of 48 to just 9. Faded nobility.

**Netha** — the world's last best hope for carnivory, three creatures at carnivory 0.43, slower and meatier than their neighbors. If this world ran another fifty thousand ticks, the wolves — if they ever came — would come from something like Netha.

## What the specimen teaches

This jar never needed a predator to be interesting. Its whole drama was a single, ruthless, beautiful gradient — *faster, smaller, sharper* — playing out through a hundred and twenty-nine named bloodlines, climbing toward a speed ceiling it had nearly hit when the clock stopped. The founder built the world and the world buried the founder. That's not a bug or a happy accident in the rules; it's just what selection does when you let it run.

If you want to see the wolves, future Tachi, we'd have to change the *world*, not the creatures — thin the plants, clump them harder, force the crowding that makes a neighbor look like a meal. The genomes are willing. The ecology has just never been desperate enough.
